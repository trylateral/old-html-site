Four day week and the art of life.

How much work is too much work?

Originally published in June 2016 on my old wordpress blog.

I sink obediently once more into my ergonomic chair. I unlock the email machine with a reflexual flick of the fingers. Spin, spin, spin goes the mouse wheel, the RSI twinge in my right hand a sharp reminder of the hours spent scrolling through reports and spreadsheets.

Let’s get this right from the start:

Unlike many people in the world, I have a regular, ‘9 to 5′ job that requires very little physical exertion and keeps me warm and dry most of the year. Whilst it doesn’t shower me with cash, I have enough to rent a roof over my head, pay my bills and still have something left over for holidays, a flashy phone, trips to the pub, and all the other trappings of modern, middle class life. I enjoy my job, most of the time at least. I am an engineer—physicist, really—whose work is helping to provide for the future in a sustainable way.

I am lucky.

And yet...

I have recently taken the decision to change from a five-day to a four-day working week. That hopefully sounds to you quite simple and uncontroversial, but I have found there is something subversive in what I am doing. Not wanting to work as much as everyone else in the office makes me a little bit odd or, at best, lazy. I was surprised to find much of my reasoning carefully set out, and in more effective prose than I might achieve, in a short essay by John Maynard Keynes from almost one hundred years ago. Let me explain:

From the depths of pre-history until very recent times, the human story was mostly one of survival. Our forebears worked from absolute—in the literal sense of the word—necessity. The need to eat, to house oneself, to clothe oneself, and to keep oneself safe from the other; wild animals, wild weather, and more often than not, wild humans.

As arch-economist Keynes predicted in his 1930 essay on the future of our prosperity[1], most of us, in the developed world at least (I’ll try to address how these ideas might apply to the developing world in a future post), are now able to meet all our “absolute needs”. Much of the remaining poverty in the developed world might be deemed relative, arising from a sense of inequality rather than a lack of essential goods, or even of basic luxuries. Inequality is an issue of itself that I don’t attempt to cover here. Social movements of the 19th and 20th century have already assured most of us a five-day working week. These two days are enough time to catch up on forfeited sleep and outstanding housework, but not much else. How about, then, working four days a week? Three days? Two?

Keynes predicted that people of the 21st century would suffer from “technological unemployment”, i.e. machines taking the jobs of humans faster than new jobs can be created, whilst still meeting all of those absolute needs I mentioned previously. He saw this underemployment (“three-hour shifts or a fifteen-hour week”) as a positive thing, envisioning a world freed from toil but enriched by arts, leisure, sport, study and other private pursuits.

What went wrong? Where is Keynes’ utopia? Whilst Keynes was right in his prediction that economic output would grow many times over as labour-saving technology spread to all industries, we have also been remarkably effective at creating new stuff to sell, i.e. creating new demand. As the supply of basic goods grew, demand for luxuries, some of which Keynes could not even have conceived, has also grown. More luxuries, more factories, more jobs, more busy workers.

In addition, the increasingly centralised, state-run world that Keynes experienced has been washed away by the tide of neoliberalism that has swept across the world since the fall of Communism. The shareholder must at all times have his return on investment. Profits must go up every year. The Chancellor of the Exchequer must meet his GDP growth forecast. Continued worldwide GDP growth ensures close to 100% employment. There are many arguments for and against such growth (reducing poverty in the developing world and environmental impacts being just two issues affected). However, before this post becomes an economics essay, let me ask that question again: Where is Keynes’ utopia?

I want to find out whether, for a lucky few of us, it is already here, waiting patiently, expectantly, just outside the window. Maybe if I break the never-ending cycle of daily commutes and weekend recoveries, if I try a third mode of living for one day a week, I might find my own, small piece of utopia. I might learn new things, travel to places I’ve never been, meet new people I’d never otherwise have met, and experience what Keynes referred to as the “art of life”:

“The strenuous purposeful money-makers may carry all of us along with them into the lap of economic abundance. But it will be those peoples, who can keep alive, and cultivate into a fuller perfection, the art of life itself and do not sell themselves for the means of life, who will be able to enjoy the abundance when it comes.”

Of course there are some sacrifices I will have to make by taking a 20% pay cut overnight, but that is kind of the point! Any sacrifice would only be the loss of a luxury, because I am lucky to be middle class and from a developed nation. Hopefully, when weighed against twenty-four extra hours of free time every week, those won’t be hard choices to make.

So what has all this got to do with this blog? This blog is, in a way, a record of what I do with my free time that one extra free day a week. It is an experiment with no objective and no desired outcome other than to have some fun along the way. To be frank, you have as much idea as I do as to where this path will lead. That, of course, is part of its charm.
